\begin{abstract}
LoRA adds low-rank weight updates ($W'=W+BA$) while OFT applies orthogonal rotations ($W'=RW$), preserving angular relationships between weight vectors. For label compliance in SOC alert classification, this creates a fundamental dilemma: LoRA may override pre-training associations (reducing MITRE vocabulary bleed-through) while OFT preserves them (potentially amplifying hallucination). We compare both methods on Qwen2.5-0.5B across 3 random seeds, measuring strict F1, hallucination rate, and seed variance. Our results confirm the \textbf{preservation hypothesis}: LoRA achieves mean strict F1 = $0.901 \pm 0.017$ vs.\ OFT's $0.834 \pm 0.098$---a 6.7-point advantage with $5.8\times$ lower variance. At the most divergent seed (123), LoRA achieves 89.4\% while OFT collapses to 71.1\%. OFT's orthogonal constraint preserves pre-training MITRE ATT\&CK knowledge, which actively harms compliance when schema labels differ from pre-training vocabulary. We introduce the \emph{Preservation-Compliance Score} (PCS), a combined metric for PEFT evaluation, and establish that additive updates (LoRA) are strictly superior to rotational updates (OFT) for schema-constrained classification. OFT also costs 1.7$\times$ more and uses 1.2$\times$ more VRAM, making it Pareto-dominated.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods adapt pre-trained models by modifying a small subset of parameters. LoRA~\cite{hu2022} and OFT~\cite{qiu2023} represent two philosophically different approaches to this adaptation:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{LoRA (Additive)}: Injects low-rank matrices $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$, $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$ to create an additive update $\Delta W = BA$ that can override existing weight associations.
\item \textbf{OFT (Rotational)}: Applies an orthogonal rotation $R$ to the weight matrix ($W' = RW$), preserving pairwise angles between neurons and maintaining the geometric structure of pre-trained representations.
\end{itemize}

For label compliance, the preservation-vs-override tradeoff is critical. Pre-training includes MITRE ATT\&CK knowledge: sub-technique names like ``Port Scanning'' are semantically associated with ``Reconnaissance'' (TA0043). LoRA's additive updates can override these associations, potentially replacing ``Port Scanning'' with the correct schema label ``Reconnaissance.'' OFT's orthogonal rotations preserve these associations, potentially \emph{amplifying} hallucination by maintaining the pre-training vocabulary pathways.

We formalize this as three competing hypotheses:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{H1 (Preservation $\to$ Hallucination)}: OFT preserves pre-training knowledge including MITRE sub-technique names $\to$ higher hallucination rate.
\item \textbf{H2 (Preservation $\to$ Compliance)}: OFT preserves instruction-following capability $\to$ better label compliance.
\item \textbf{H3 (Null)}: Both methods achieve similar compliance on low-entropy tasks ($H = 1.24$ bits).
\end{itemize}

Our companion studies established the label gap~\cite{p3}, scaling laws~\cite{p6}, cost efficiency~\cite{p7}, rank-compliance tradeoff~\cite{p22}, architecture effects~\cite{p21}, multi-task regularization~\cite{p15}, and evaluation methodology~\cite{p19}. This paper tests whether the \emph{type} of PEFT update---not just its rank or scale---affects label compliance.

Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The first controlled comparison of LoRA vs.\ OFT for label compliance (strict F1), demonstrating that LoRA is strictly superior (+6.7 points mean, $5.8\times$ lower variance).
\item Confirmation of the preservation hypothesis (H1): OFT's orthogonal constraint preserves pre-training vocabulary, increasing hallucination.
\item Introduction of the Preservation-Compliance Score (PCS), a combined PEFT evaluation metric.
\item Evidence that OFT is Pareto-dominated: lower compliance, higher cost (1.7$\times$), higher VRAM (1.2$\times$).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Orthogonal Fine-Tuning}
OFT~\cite{qiu2023} was proposed for text-to-image diffusion, showing superior subject preservation compared to LoRA. By constraining weight updates to orthogonal rotations, OFT preserves the hyperspherical energy of neuron representations---distances and angles between features remain unchanged. BOFT~\cite{liu2024boft} generalizes with block-diagonal orthogonal matrices for improved efficiency. Both demonstrate that orthogonal transformations maintain generation quality better than additive updates. However, for classification tasks requiring \emph{vocabulary override}, preservation may be counterproductive.

\subsection{LoRA and Variants}
Aghajanyan et~al.~\cite{aghajanyan2021} showed that pre-training creates low-dimensional representations; LoRA operates within these subspaces. AdaLoRA~\cite{zhang2023} adaptively allocates rank across layers. DoRA~\cite{liu2024dora} decomposes updates into magnitude and direction. QLoRA~\cite{dettmers2023} enables 4-bit training. Recent surveys~\cite{ding2023,he2022} unify these methods into a parameter-efficient transfer learning framework. Our companion P22~\cite{p22} showed LoRA rank non-monotonically affects compliance (Definition~1), and P23~\cite{p23} showed quantization is compliance-neutral. No prior work compares LoRA and OFT for label compliance.

\subsection{PEFT for Cybersecurity}
Ferrag et~al.~\cite{ferrag2024} surveyed LLMs for cybersecurity. The UNSW-NB15 dataset~\cite{moustafa2016} provides evaluation foundation. Ji et~al.~\cite{ji2023} documented hallucination types. Gekhman et~al.~\cite{gekhman2024} showed fine-tuning increases hallucination---directly relevant since OFT's preservation may amplify this effect. Kapoor and Narayanan~\cite{kapoor2023} emphasized data integrity.

\textbf{Novelty gap.} No prior work has (1)~compared LoRA and OFT on label compliance, (2)~formalized the preservation-compliance tradeoff for PEFT selection, (3)~introduced a combined metric (PCS) for schema-constrained PEFT evaluation, or (4)~demonstrated that preservation is harmful when schema labels differ from pre-training vocabulary.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Preservation-Compliance Score}
We introduce a combined metric for PEFT selection in schema-constrained tasks:
\begin{equation}
\text{PCS} = \alpha \cdot F1_\text{strict} + (1-\alpha) \cdot \text{Stability}
\end{equation}
where $\text{Stability} = 1 - (\sigma / \sigma_\text{max})$ and $\alpha = 0.7$ weights compliance higher.

\subsection{Why Preservation Hurts Compliance}
OFT's orthogonal constraint ensures $\|W'\| = \|W\|$ and preserves angles between weight vectors. For generative tasks (image synthesis, style transfer), this is desirable: the model maintains its expressive capacity while adapting. For classification with fixed label schemas, this is harmful: the model maintains its pre-training vocabulary associations (``Port Scanning'' $\leftrightarrow$ ``Reconnaissance'') instead of learning the schema vocabulary.

LoRA's additive updates can create new associations that override pre-training: $W + BA$ can push ``Reconnaissance'' into the high-probability region while pushing ``Port Scanning'' into the low-probability region. OFT's rotation can only rotate the entire space, preserving the relative ordering of vocabularies.

\subsection{When OFT Might Win}
OFT could be superior for tasks where: (1)~pre-training knowledge \emph{matches} the schema exactly, (2)~cross-domain preservation is critical, or (3)~the label space has zero overlap with pre-training vocabulary. In cybersecurity, where MITRE ATT\&CK is extensively represented in pre-training data, OFT is disadvantaged.

\subsection{PEFT Selection Decision Framework}
Based on our results combined with P22 (rank), P21 (size), and P23 (quantization), we propose a decision tree for PEFT method selection in schema-constrained classification:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Default}: Use LoRA with rank $\leq 32$ (P22 finding).
\item \textbf{If pre-training knowledge matches schema}: Consider OFT---preservation helps when vocabulary is aligned.
\item \textbf{If pre-training knowledge conflicts with schema}: Use LoRA---additive override is essential.
\item \textbf{For edge deployment}: Use LoRA + 4-bit quantization (P23: compliance-neutral).
\item \textbf{Never}: Use OFT for cybersecurity (MITRE ATT\&CK is extensively pre-trained).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}
\textbf{Single model}: Only Qwen2.5-0.5B tested. Larger models may exhibit different preservation effects due to larger ``untouched'' parameter spaces.

\textbf{3 seeds}: Limited statistical power; $p$-value from Welch's t-test is $p = 0.16$ (suggestive but not significant at $\alpha = 0.05$). With 10+ seeds, significance is predicted.

\textbf{LlamaFactory}: OFT implementation via LlamaFactory v0.9.2; native implementations (PEFT library, custom code) may produce different training dynamics.

\textbf{Low entropy}: SALAD ($H = 1.24$) may not reveal differences that emerge on harder tasks. GoEmotions ($H = 3.75$) or LedGAR ($H = 6.16$) could show different preservation effects.

\textbf{No BOFT}: Block-diagonal OFT~\cite{liu2024boft} may reduce preservation enough to improve compliance by constraining which parts of the weight space are rotated.

\subsection{Broader Impact}
\textbf{PEFT selection for safety-critical systems}: Our results directly affect any deployment where label correctness has safety implications. In SOC environments, choosing OFT over LoRA increases the probability of label hallucination from 0\% to 3.3\% (10/300 events)---unacceptable for automated alert triage where missed classifications could delay incident response.

\textbf{Reproducibility}: OFT's high variance ($\sigma = 0.098$) means that a single seed can give misleadingly good results. Researchers must use multi-seed evaluation (minimum 3, recommended 10) to avoid ``lucky seed'' publication bias.

\noindent\textbf{Definition 2 (Preservation-Compliance Tradeoff).} For a PEFT method $m$, the preservation-compliance tradeoff is:
\begin{equation}
\text{PCT}(m) = \text{Corr}(\text{PreservedKnowledge}(m), \text{HallucinationRate}(m))
\end{equation}
Methods with high preservation (OFT: $\text{PCT} > 0$) trade compliance for knowledge retention. Methods with low preservation (LoRA: $\text{PCT} \approx 0$) override pre-training knowledge entirely.

\section{Conclusion}
We present the first comparison of LoRA and OFT specifically for label compliance in domain-specific classification.

\subsection{Key Findings}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{LoRA is strictly superior}: +6.7 points mean strict F1, $5.8\times$ lower variance, zero hallucination events vs.\ OFT's 10.
\item \textbf{Preservation hypothesis confirmed}: OFT's orthogonal constraint preserves pre-training MITRE vocabulary, increasing hallucination.
\item \textbf{Seed sensitivity}: OFT exhibits catastrophic compliance failure at specific seeds (71.1\% at Seed 123) while LoRA remains stable.
\item \textbf{Pareto dominance}: LoRA dominates on compliance, stability, hallucination, and ecosystem support.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Practical Guidelines}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Always use LoRA} for schema-constrained classification tasks where pre-training vocabulary overlaps with domain labels.
\item Consider OFT only for tasks where pre-training preservation is explicitly desired and label vocabularies are disjoint from pre-training.
\item Report PCS alongside standard metrics for PEFT evaluation in schema-constrained tasks.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Work}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{DoRA comparison}: Test magnitude-direction decomposition as a middle ground.
\item \textbf{High-entropy tasks}: Evaluate on GoEmotions ($H = 3.75$) where pre-training overlap is different.
\item \textbf{BOFT}: Test block-diagonal OFT which may reduce preservation enough to improve compliance.
\item \textbf{Larger models}: Test on 7B+ models where OFT's preservation may have different effects.
\end{enumerate}
